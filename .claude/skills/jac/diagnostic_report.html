Priority Action Plan

Action 1 — Highest Priority
Science Vocabulary (5 wrong)
Wrong: Q6 (90% cohort correct), Q7 (70%), Q15 (86%), Q28 (53%), Q30 (87%)
Q6, Q15, and Q30 were missed even though 86–90% of the cohort got them right — these are "should-know" questions, not hard ones. The student likely knows the topic loosely but is tripped up by similar-sounding science terms (e.g. photosynthesis vs. respiration, vertebrate vs. invertebrate).
Do today: Make 20 flashcards for Year 5 science terms. Learn 5 new cards each day + review yesterday's. Focus: biology terms (living systems), earth science vocabulary.
Action 2 — High Priority
Synonyms (0/2) & Analogies (0/1)
Wrong: Q4 (Synonym, 49% cohort), Q5 (Analogies, 28%), Q27 (Synonym, 52%)
Q5 Analogies was the hardest question in the test (only 28% of students correct) — missing it is expected. But Q4 and Q27 are mid-difficulty synonyms that most students get wrong too. The weakness here is vocabulary breadth: not knowing enough word pairs to make quick synonym judgements under timed pressure.
Do today: Write 10 synonym pairs + 5 analogy pairs each morning before school. Use a Year 5–6 word list. Focus on "X is to Y as ___ is to ___" format for analogies.
Action 3 — High Priority
Palindromes (0/2)
Wrong: Q23 (51% cohort), Q24 (82% cohort got right)
Q24 was missed by only 18% of students — this is a well-understood concept for most Year 5 students. A palindrome reads the same forwards and backwards (e.g. "racecar", "level", "noon"). The student may not have memorised the rule firmly or be confusing palindromes with anagrams. This is a fast fix.
Do today: Memorise the definition: "A palindrome reads the same forwards AND backwards." Learn 15 common palindrome words. Practice spotting them in a list.
Action 4 — Improve
Idioms & Colloquialisms
Wrong: Q11 (Idioms, 77%), Q21 (Colloquialisms, 52%)
Q11 was an idiom that 77% of students got right — a well-known expression the student didn't recognise. Australian and British idioms (e.g. "barking up the wrong tree", "kick the bucket") appear regularly in JAC tests and are best learnt through exposure and repetition rather than rules.
Do today: Learn 5 new Australian/British idioms each week. Write each in a sentence. Keep an "idiom journal." Include colloquialisms like "fortnight", "arvo", "reckon".
